Description

Aims and Fit of Module(i.e. relationship to programme)
To extend students’ general and discipline-specific English language and study skills, with an emphasis on critical thinking and independent learning, in order to allow them to function more effectively in other university modules, as well as in their overall academic career, and to raise their language skills to CEFR (Common European Framework of Reference for Languages) B2+ level, with a general level of ability that is broadly comparable to SEEC (Southern England Consortium for Credit Accumulation and Transfer) Level 4.
Learning Outcomes(for accreditation and other reasons,sub-headings could be added to this section)
Students completing the module successfully should be able to:
A.Employ diverse listening and note-taking strategies to effectively enable general, detailed and critical understanding of discipline specific and general academic spoken texts.

B.Employ diverse reading and note-taking strategies to effectively enable general, detailed and critical understanding of discipline specific readings and general academic texts.

C.Demonstrate a range of speaking skills to describe, explain, summarize and critically analyze topics relevant to their field of study with a high degree of fluency and an appropriate academic style (when required) and lexical register.

D.Employ a range of writing skills to describe, explain, summarize and critically analyze topics relevant to their field of study with an appropriate academic style and lexical register.

E.Demonstrate competence in adhering to academic integrity requirements.
Method of Teaching and Learning
Following the practice of University of Liverpool’s English Language Centre, materials, instructional design and assessments will be framed according to the Academic Literacies approach. This means foregoing a one-size fits all study skills perspective on EAP in recognition of the fact that departments have their own set of academic literacy practices, in reading and writing particularly, that students need help understanding. To that end, materials

and assessments will be developed in consultation/collaboration with department academic staff. Smaller class groupings will allow for increased and sustained formative feedback which is both responsive and supportive of students’ needs. 



Students will have four hours of classroom contact time (seminars) and seven hours of self-study per week. The seminars will be conducted using various EAP teaching approaches and methodologies that encourage students to discover meaning and knowledge independently and by working with peers. Students will also be given guidance on, and opportunities to practice the various skills mentioned in the learning outcomes. The self-study activities will be extensions and/or consolidation of work carried out in the seminars. 



No more than two classes per semester (four hours) will be replaced with one-on-one or group tutorials.



Speaking coursework assessments normally take place during normal teaching weeks.
Syllabus
SKILLS: 

Academic skills and strategies to be taught will be considered in consultation with departments and may include:



Writing

•	demonstrating aspects of critical thinking in academic writing

•	reading responses (verbally and in writing)

•	summary writing

•	writing paraphrase

•	literature reviews

•	interpretive writing

•	reflective writing

•	lab reports (for civil engineering students)

•	critical analysis 

•	reviewing/practising language for comparison and contrast

•	reviewing/practising language for describing causes and effects

•	reviewing/practising language for discussing problems, solutions and evaluation



Reading

•	identifying characteristics of written genres from the disciplines

•	critical reading skills

•	approaches to intensive and extensive reading

•	reading deeply

•	reading and interpreting complex instructions e.g. on CW briefs and exam questions

•	reading and interpreting complex visual sources e.g. posters, graphs/charts/tables/illustrations in textbooks and other academic sources

•	identifying author’s stance

•	deductive understanding



Listening

•	effective involvement in class tutorial discussions

•	evaluating, organizing and predicting listening content

•	participation in lecture listening (beyond just simply note-taking)

•	active listening skills



Speaking

•	effective involvement in class tutorial discussions

•	effective group work skills

•	building “English self” confidence and fluency by conversing in English on everyday topics 

•	presenting in English on academic topics – group / individual

•	speaking and using visuals e.g. PPT

•	expressing aspects of critical thinking in speaking

•	responding to scrutiny of own work/ideas from peers and teachers

•	evaluating and reflecting on completed work

•	techniques of drama in English

•	advanced pronunciation skills: rhythm, stress, intonation

Other

•	design principles of good academic posters

•	strategies for learning and practising subject specific vocabulary in discipline specific learning contexts



Study Skills

•	using Vancouver or Harvard referencing system in written assignments

•	understanding and using feedback to improve written and oral work

•	finding and using sources for academic writing e.g. case studies

o	beyond just avoiding plagiarism, why are sources important

o	types of sources, credibility of sources

o	searching for sources – library skills

o	the structure of sources (AL perspective)

o	selecting information appropriately from sources

o	deciding to paraphrase, summarize or quote from sources



TOPICS:

Topics may include:

•	well-known planners, architects and engineers 

•	the five impacts: how built environment projects impact on the areas around them culturally, environmentally, economically, geographically and socially

•	materials e.g. metal that breathes

•	structures e.g. skyscrapers
